Hub and cluster

     This website is a hub website that serves as an introduction to a cluster of six websites. It presents the theoretical presuppositions on which the system is built, and describes the nature of the websites that are included in the cluster. It is also written in a style that is intedned specifically for a narrative, browser oriented, website.
     While theoretical in nature, this hub website is primarily intended to serve as an introdution to the use of the websites in the cluster. For a full articulation of the theory I refer to the volume A Critique of Archaeological Reason, especially Part IV.

Substantive

     The primary aim of the Four Banks cluster of websites is substantive: the focus is on content, on the way in which data are organized, on the information they provide, on the argument that is built on them.
     In so doing, we study the hermeneutic process behind each of the six domains represented in the websites, aiming for a description of, and a confrontation with, the scholarship that has, over the last two centuries, documented the data and shaped our mental categories towards them.
     My intent is to produce more than bibliographical tool, though the websites will be that as well. On the one hand, each website engages in a thematic critique with the literature, while at the same time developing an overall substantive discourse that affects the core of the wesite's domain (archaeology, religion, etc.).
     One will find a fuller description of this aim in Part 3 of this website, as shown in the side bar to the left.

Methodological

     A major collateral aim of the project is methodological. I aim to implement a distinctive digital approach to scholarly publishing by developing a close interlacing of data with a core argument and with related themes that are produced as natively digital.
     To this end, I stress the importance of framing a website within a well defined structural whole, through a firm commitment to implementing the notions of digital discourse and digital thought.
     This leads to more far-reaching implications about the very nature of digital humanism and its relationship to the social sciences. Gaining distance by looking at the whole, one gains at the same time an unexpected, controlled closeness to the substance of argument and data.
     One will find a detailed description of this aim in the METHODOLOGY section of this website as shown in the side bar to the left.
