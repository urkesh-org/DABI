Two structures

     We may distinguish two basic publishing structures through which knowledge is constructed and accessed: analog and digital.
     An analog structure is the one well establihed in scholarship: it is physically embodied in a paper publication, which may also appear in an equivalent digital format (typically .PDF). It presents the reader with a closed whole, wherein the argument and the data are laid out in static juxtaposition, with the argument flowing in a unilinear path.
     A digital structure, on the other hand, presents the reader with an open aggregate, wherein the argument and the data are dynamically interacting along multilinear paths that can be accessed concurrently. I view websites as the digital correlatives of paper publications, and the 4Banks websites aim at implementing this approach.
     The two structures share the same conceptual orientation, and are to be seen not in opposition to each other, but rather in a relationhsip of reciprocal synergy.

Modalities

     The two structures parallel each other closely, but with different modalities. There is, we may say, a bimodal structure, in the sense that the data as presented in the websites are designed so as to be accessed in parallel ways depending on the intent with which one approaches the website. In other words, each website is so structured as to serve the two stated aims (substantive and methodological) in ways that are consonant but distinct.
     The four modalities are: continuity, contiguity, connectivity and sequentiality. They are realized differentially for each of the strutures as shown in the following diagram, and as detailed further in the paragraphs that follow.

1. Continuity: closed vs. open

     In an analog structure, the data are seen as a continuum, not only formally (as on a printed page), but also conceptually. The flow of the argument takes place within the bounds of this continuum, and both writer and reader are encased, as it were, within it. In this sense, the system is closed, tightly packed: the succession of steps that form the argument is self-contained, what can be added can only come from the outside of the finished product.
     In a digital structure, on the other hand, the data are both continuous and not: there is a succession of the elements of the argument, but the steps in the flow are seen primarily as such, i. e., as steps which derive their continuity from being juxtaposed, but remain essentially isolated. It is for this reason that quantification is the hallmark of the digital approach: the fragments are so structured that they can be seen in and by themselves.

2. Contiguity: whole vs. aggregate

     In an analog structure, the data are seen as tightly contiguous with each other. We tend to look at the argument in its integrity as a whole, which also follows from the modality of continuity: the perception of the whole is possible because the structure is closed.. A telltale sign of the system is that we can see the resulting product "from cover to cover," which applies also to a .PDF version.
     In a digital structure, on the other hand, the data are both contiguous and not: on the one hand they are encased within the same type of argument flow that applies to the analog structure, while on the other the argument emerges more as an aggregate of disparate elements than as a whole, and we tend to see the argument in this light, precisely as the sum of its parts. The positional dimension of each element is constitutive of its identity.

3. Connectivity: static vs. dynamic

     In an analog structure, connectivity among data is potential, hence static. The essential characteristics of critical judgment consists in bracing the disparate elements within the continuum of the argument flow. In an analog structure, this bracing happens in the mind of the reader, who can "jump" back and forth, as well as outside of, the argument flow, on the strength of his or her own knowledge. The elements of the argument, the moments in its flow, as well as the data on which it is based, are there to be seen; but they are juxtaposed statically.
     In a digital structure, instead, bracing is inherent in the very structural make-up of the data. Connectivity among the moments of the argument flow, as well as the data on which the argument rests, is built into these elements, moments and data, which are thus linked dynamically with each other.

4. Sequentiality: unilinear vs. multilinear

     A unilinear argument follows a single thread. On a printed page, footnotes point to parallel threads, without however following them in their entirety. There are also parallel threads that are developed more explicitly, in the form of excursuses and appendices. But these are generally limited in scope, and more difficult to see concurrently with the main thread of the central argument. Sequentiality is thus tightly tied to the flow of the argument.
     A multilinear approach, on the other hand, is so structured as to propose concurrent threads that run simultaneously. Footnotes do not properly exist because they are raised to the level of the primary argument, and so are excursuses and appendices. We have a multi-tiered construct where a single argument flows along synchronous tracks, as if producing a harmony out of distinct, but consonant contributing elements.

Websites

           If a printed book, monograph or article is the primary example of an analog publication system, a website may be seen as the primary example of a digital publication system.
     Current scholarly websites fall typically in two categories.
     (1) A data base model, where data are presented according do complex categorization systems, allowing unlimited queries and sorts. Conceptually, this may be seen as the correlative of a dictionary or, at best, of an encyclopaedia.
      (2) A separate narrative, typically in a .PDF format, which have two

Analogies

     analog clock face: whole as a gradient
     By way of comparison, an analog thermometer consists of an element, such as mercury, which expands in correspondence to the temperature with which it is in contact: continuity is here found in the way in which the element, mercury, expands, and the measure is set through a preordained gauge. – At the other end, we may think of a digital picture, which is made up of individual pixels that can be identified as such, as when we speak of the DPI (dots per inch) factor.
     By way of comparison, we may think of the difference between looking at the picture of a landscape and actually driving through it. The picture will evoke memories of other pictures, which may be produced to favor a critical confrontation: the picture in itself is static, and the dynamics of the comparison is introduced by the observer. – The drive through, on the other hand, is dynamic by its very nature: it unfolds a series of views, which correspond to the pictures one could view statically, except that here they move of their own accord in and out of the perceptual range of the observer, presenting built-in comparisons as one moves forward.
     By way of comparison, we may think of a song, where the melody flows carried by a single voice. We may focus on the tune or the lyrics, we may reproduce the melody on an instrument, we may consider isolated segments but the fundamental sequentiality of the single human voice remains essentially unilinear in its structure. – At the other end, we may think of a symphony or better yet of a concerto for orchestra and a solo instrument, where parallel registers flow along together within the compass of a single unified score. We may say that a properly digital text is structured along the lines of a conceptual harmony that emulates that of an orchestral ensemble.
